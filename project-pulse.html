Pulse: Reimagining Emergency Triage

A self-triage kiosk that bridges patients and providers through clarity, empathy, and intelligent design.

Role Lead Product & Service Designer
Duration 6 Weeks
Team 4 Designers
Type Proof of Concept / UX Research & Prototype
Tools Figma, Miro, Canva

Emergency departments run under constant pressure: long waits, chaotic workflows, and anxious patients. Pulse started with a simple question: what if the first touchpoint in an emergency room eased stress instead of adding to it? We designed a self-triage kiosk that standardizes intake, empowers patients with transparency, and gives medical staff the structured data they need to make faster, smarter decisions.

The Challenge

Problem

Long intake times and unclear triage processes

Patients arriving at emergency departments face manual intake processes that are slow, inconsistent, and opaque. Without clear communication about wait times or priority, anxiety builds while staff manually record symptoms across different systems.

Impact

Bottlenecks and inefficiency for everyone

Manual data entry adds an average of 127 seconds per patient. Inconsistent symptom recording leads to triage variability. Patients perceive longer waits when they lack information, while staff operate reactively without visibility into incoming volume.

Goal

Simplify check-in while maintaining human connection

Design a self-triage system that collects standardized data quickly and accurately, provides transparent feedback to patients, and integrates seamlessly into existing ED workflows, all while keeping the essential human touch that healthcare requires.

Healthcare Professional Validation

We presented Pulse to two healthcare professionals with emergency department experience. Both confirmed its feasibility for pilot testing and appreciated its potential to reduce early-stage bottlenecks and cognitive load on triage nurses without compromising care quality.

Design Process

From bodystorming to high-fidelity prototypes, we iterated through multiple rounds of testing to ensure Pulse worked in the high-stress, high-stakes environment of an emergency department.

Physical Exploration

We physically acted out the check-in process, tapping through mock screens, scanning health cards, and printing tickets. This hands-on exploration revealed critical usability gaps:

  • Progress indicators ("Step 1 of 4") were essential to reduce anxiety
  • Card-scan icon looked like a static graphic, not an interactive element
  • Missing back-navigation and confirmation states caused confusion

Paper Prototyping

Paper prototypes let us simulate the flow and observe real user behavior without investing in high-fidelity design. Key learnings:

  • Added step confirmations and "Back" buttons throughout
  • Tested different screen heights and text contrast for accessibility
  • Identified where users expected touch feedback vs. automatic progression

Interactive Prototype

Using Figma, we built a complete interactive system that could be tested in realistic scenarios:

  • Translated findings into a cohesive visual language
  • Designed for high-stress moments with soft blues, high contrast, and minimal text
  • Added audio and visual confirmation cues for every interaction

Design System

A consistent visual language designed for stress, accessibility, and clarity in high-stakes healthcare environments.

Reflection & Next Steps

What I Learned

Service Design in Healthcare

This project reinforced that successful service design happens where digital experiences meet real-world behavior. Context is everything, and what works in a coffee shop will never fly in an emergency room.

Every design decision had to account for stress, lighting, mobility constraints, and split-second urgency. Bodystorming revealed issues that wouldn't have surfaced in traditional wireframing.

Future Directions

If we were to continue development, the next priorities would be:

  • Comprehensive accessibility testing for mobility and visual impairments
  • Staff dashboard with crowding trends and AI-driven forecasts
  • Integration testing with existing hospital management systems
  • Extended pilot in a real ED environment with diverse patient populations

Outcome: Pulse remains a validated proof of concept and shows how thoughtful UX and service design can deliver real operational and emotional improvements in healthcare. While not deployed in practice, it has been showcased as a model of how empathy and efficiency can coexist in high-stress environments.
