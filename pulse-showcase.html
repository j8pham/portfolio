Wait... what happens here? Uncertainty at every step.

The journey looks simple on paper, but in reality each step is full of uncertainty. The biggest gap: there's no clear, guided starting point that works for both patients and staff.

Key Insights

What we heard

From our research, three themes kept coming up: Patients are hesitant and overwhelmed. Forms are confusing and often incomplete. Staff lose time fixing that, instead of focusing on care. Those became the backbone for our design direction.

People are scared to bother staff with 'small' questions.
Forms come back incomplete or illegible.
Triage decisions are delayed by missing information.
Opportunity

Our opportunity and design pillars

Create a guided check-in experience that reduces anxiety for patients and delivers clean, actionable information for staff.

Calm Under Pressure
No One Left Guessing
Help Staff Help Faster
Accessible by Default

To stay focused, we defined four design pillars. We used them as a filter during every decision and iteration. If a screen didn't support these, we changed it.

Act III: The Solution

Pulse: a calmer way to check in

Pulse is a check-in kiosk placed at the ER entrance. It guides patients through a short, focused flow, flags critical information for staff, and clearly tells patients what will happen next. Throughout the design process, we had to conform to hospital standard design by following their simple design system and keeping it inline with their current systems.

Pillar 1

Reduce cognitive load

  • Large primary action for clarity
  • Visible language options immediately
  • Clear emergency bypass option

To reduce cognitive load, the first screen had to be obvious and reassuring.

  • ‚úì One dominant action for clarity
  • ‚úì Visible language options immediately available
  • ‚úì Clear line for life-threatening cases to never block urgent care
  • ‚úì Simple, high-contrast layout for stressed and tired people
Pillar 2

Show status & next steps

To make sure no one feels lost, every step shows progress and next steps.

  • ‚úì Shows where you are in the process
  • ‚úì Questions broken into small chunks with plain language
  • ‚úì Each screen focused on a single decision
Pillar 3

Structure info for triage

For staff, we prioritized structured information over generic form dumps.

  • ‚úì Risk flags automatically tagged and surfaced
  • ‚úì Critical symptoms like chest pain highlighted immediately
  • ‚úì Integrates with existing triage tools for quick action

We designed with accessibility as a starting point, not an add-on. Large targets, high contrast, and plain language support people who are older, in pain, or in a rush. We also had to conform to hospital standard design by following their simple design system and keeping it inline with their current systems, ensuring consistency across all hospital interfaces. In future iterations, I'd push this further with screen reader support, height considerations for the physical kiosk, and more language options.

Act IV: Results & Iteration

How the pillars changed the design

Before

  • Busy screens
  • Long paragraphs of text
  • Overwhelming options

After

  • ‚úì Simplified flows
  • ‚úì Clear progress indicators
  • ‚úì One question per screen

These pillars weren't just theory. Here's a quick example: in our first version, we tried to collect everything on one screen. When we evaluated it against reducing cognitive load and showing clear status and next steps, we realized it was overwhelming. So we split the flow into smaller steps, added progress, and simplified the copy. Each iteration passed through the lens of: does this reduce stress and help staff act faster? Along the way, we worked within hospital design standards, following their simple design system to ensure seamless integration with existing systems.

Impact

What we heard and why it matters

When we showed this to healthcare professionals, they appreciated that the kiosk wasn't replacing their judgment, just reducing admin noise. They liked the structured intake and emphasis on clarity. Even as a concept, we see potential for: cleaner data, less repeated questioning, and patients feeling more confident they're 'in the system'.

"This doesn't replace my judgment, it just reduces the admin noise."
"I like that the data comes in structured. No more bad handwriting."
